Implications of county-level variation in U.S. opioid distribution

Abstract

Background

Prescription opioids accounted for the majority of opioid-related deaths in the United States prior to 2010, and continue to contribute to opioid misuse and mortality. We used a novel dataset to investigate the distributional patterns of prescription opioids, whether opioid pill volume was associated with opioid-related mortality, and whether early state Medicaid expansions were associated with either pill volume or opioid-related mortality.

Methods

Data on opioid shipments to retail pharmacies for 2006–2013 were obtained from the U.S. Drug Enforcement Administration, and opioid-related deaths (ORDs) were obtained from the Centers for Disease Control and Prevention. We first compared characteristics of counties in the highest and lowest quartiles for per capita pill volume (PCPV). We used adjusted difference-in-differences regression models to identify factors associated with PCPV or ORDs, and whether early state Medicaid expansions were associated with either outcome. All models were estimated as linear regressions with standard errors clustered by county, and weighted by county population.

Results

We found large geographic variations in opioid distribution, and this variation appears to be driven by differences in demographics, healthcare access, and healthcare supply. In adjusted models, a one-pill increase in PCPV was associated with a 0.20 increase in ORDs per 100,000 population (95 % CI 0.11–0.30). Early Medicaid expansions were associated with lower PCPV (−2.20, 95 % CI −2.97 to −1.43).

Conclusions

Our findings validate the relationship between PCPV and ORDs, identify important environmental drivers of the opioid epidemic, and suggest early state Medicaid expansions were beneficial in reducing opioid pill volume.

Introduction

The effects of the opioid epidemic in the United States have been substantial. In 2019, drug overdose was the leading cause of injury-related death in the United Sates, and the majority of deaths involved prescription opioids, heroin, or fentanyl (National Academies of Sciences and Medicine, 2019). In 2018, an estimated 2.1 million people had opioid use disorder and 10.3 million people misused prescription opioids (SAMHSA, 2019). While heroin and fentanyl account for a disproportionate amount of opioid-related deaths (ORD), prescription opioids continue to be important drivers of the opioid epidemic. In 2018, 1.9 million people initiated opioid misuse through legally-obtained prescriptions (SAMHSA, 2019). These worrying trends continue to be evident despite nationally-distributed best practice prescribing guidelines and the widespread uptake of prescription drug monitoring programs to curb inappropriate prescribing (Jones et al., 2018).
Understanding the origins of the epidemic are critical to prevent further growth. Research suggests that massive increases in opioid prescribing began in the 1990s when OxyContin was introduced to the market, and sparked the epidemic we struggle to curtail today (Kolodny et al., 2015). Simultaneously, pharmaceutical industry-sponsored research reported that physical dependence and addiction were different phenomena, and that the addictive potential of prescription opioids was low (Kolodny et al., 2015). Concurrently, a focus on patient centered care was coming to the forefront of medicine (Zgierska et al., 2012). Patient satisfaction is a prominent component in measures of patient-centered care, and effective pain management is highly correlated with satisfaction (Zgierska et al., 2012). The combination of organization and patient-level pressures may have led to excessive, routine opioid administration and prescribing practices. The push for proactive pain management and lack of preparedness to treat an influx of patients with opioid use disorder may have set the stage for the present epidemic.
Increases in opioid prescribing from the 1990s to 2010 cued policy efforts to curtail excess prescribing, but significant geographic variation in opioid prescribing persists. An assessment of opioid prescriptions dispensed by retail pharmacies in 2008 found that higher opioid prescribing rates were associated with higher proportions of white residents and residents living below the federal poverty limit, but not with the burden of illness (McDonald et al., 2012). A more recent examination of county-level opioid prescriptions in 2015 and 2017 documented reductions in opioid prescribing, but significant variation persisted at the county-level (Guy et al., 2019). While research has examined correlates of variation in opioid prescribing, little is known about county-level prescribing patterns from the late-2000s through the mid-2010s when efforts to address the opioid epidemic became a focus of health policy. A Centers for Disease Control and Prevention (CDC) report in 2018 highlights that national opioid prescribing rates began to decline in 2010 and a concomitant increase was observed in synthetic opioid (i.e. heroin, fentanyl) overdose rates in the following years (Hoots et al., 2018). This phenomenon is partially attributable to discontinuation of high-dose, long-term opioid prescriptions and the risk of transitions to synthetic opioid use, which carry greater risk of overdose (Mark and Parish, 2019). The effects of opioid prescribing on fatal opioid overdose may take several years to fully realize, and there is a dearth of evidence capturing this shift at the county-level. Evaluating county-level characteristics associated with opioid prescribing may help elucidate the origins of hotspots of ORDs.
Moreover, the role of early Medicaid expansion in prescribing and ORDs remains unclear. As of 2013, five states (CA, CT, MN, NJ, WA) plus the District of Columbia were early adopters of Medicaid expansion and increased their income eligibility limitations for adults (Kaiser Family Foundation, 2012). Despite evidence that Medicaid expansion has improved access to behavioral healthcare services in multiple states (McCarty et al., 2019; Saloner et al., 2018; Shover et al., 2019), recent Congressional hearings and a U.S. Department of Homeland Security report have questioned the association between Medicaid expansion and opioid misuse (Johnson, 2018). Using a quasi-experimental design, Cher et al. (2019) find that Medicaid expansion did not have a significant effect on opioid prescribing rates in expansion versus non-expansion states. The results of this research were robust to sensitivity analyses allowing for early Medicaid-expansion, but the magnitude of the relationship is not reported (Cher et al., 2019). Additionally, early state Medicaid -expansions were also found to be potentially protective of ORDs, but these results have yet to be replicated (Venkataramani and Chatterjee, 2019). Notably, these studies relied on state-level estimates of the association between Medicaid expansion and ORDs. Additional less-aggregated evaluations of the association between early Medicaid expansion, ORDs, and the mechanisms between them (e.g. PCPV) are critical to further dispel concerns that expansion accelerated the opioid epidemic.
The Washington Post (WaPo) recently gained access to the U.S. Drug Enforcement Administration (DEA)’s Automation of Reports and Consolidated Orders System (ARCOS) pill shipment database (The Washington Post, 2019). This novel dataset provides an unprecedented opportunity to evaluate the association between opioid pill distribution and ORDs over time. Previous research using ARCOS (Kropp et al., 2019; Lin et al., 2019; Modarai et al., 2013; Paulozzi et al., 2011) has used the less-detailed, publicly-available DEA annual reports. These reports only give drug distribution data by 3-digit zip-code region, which precludes analysis of county-level variation and limits interpretation of results (Drug Enforcement Agency, 2020). This study used the novel, granular ARCOS pill shipment database (WaPo ARCOS extract) to assess the distribution patterns of prescription opioids, if opioid pill volume was associated with ORDs, and whether early state Medicaid expansions were associated with either pill volume or opioid-related mortality.

    • An external exposome-wide association study of opioid use disorder diagnosed during pregnancy in Florida

      2023, Science of the Total Environment
      Citation Excerpt :

      The distribution of opioid use varies spatiotemporally, and the variation in use is often driven by differences in the social (e.g., unemployment rate and neighborhood deprivation) and built (e.g., walkability and green space) environments. Multiple studies have shown that opioid use and related adverse outcomes such as opioid overdose and NOWS are associated with county-level factors, including median income and the unemployment rate (Blanco and Volkow, 2019; Patrick et al., 2019; Venkataramani et al., 2020), educational attainment, and access to healthcare (e.g., mental health clinician shortages) (Griffith et al., 2021; Patrick et al., 2019). Geographic disparities in access to medication for opioid use disorder (MOUD) such as buprenorphine and methadone also exist, varying by rurality and deprivation (Amiri et al., 2021).

      The prevalence of opioid use disorder (OUD) during pregnancy has quadrupled in recent years and widely varies geographically in the US. However, few studies have examined which environmental factors are associated with OUD during pregnancy. We conducted an external exposome-wide association study (ExWAS) to investigate the associations between external environmental factors and OUD diagnosed during pregnancy. Data were obtained from a unique, statewide database in Florida comprising linked individual-level birth and electronic health records. A total of 255,228 pregnancies with conception dates between 2012 and 2016 were included. We examined 82 exposome measures characterizing seven aspects of the built and social environment and spatiotemporally linked them to each individual record. A two-phase procedure was utilized for the external ExWAS. In Phase 1, we randomly divided the data into a discovery set (50 %) and a replication set (50 %). Associations between exposome measures (normalized and standardized) and OUD initially diagnosed during pregnancy were examined using logistic regression. A total of 15 variables were significant in both the discovery and replication sets. In Phase 2, multivariable logistic regression was used to fit all variables selected from Phase 1. Measures of walkability (the national walkability index, OR: 1.23, 95 % CI: 1.17, 1.29), vacant land (the percent vacant land for 36 months or longer, OR: 1.06, 95 % CI: 1.00, 1.12) and food access (the percentage of low food access population that are seniors at 1/2 mile, OR: 1.47, 95 % CI: 1.38, 1.57) were each associated with diagnosis of OUD during pregnancy. This is the first external ExWAS of OUD during pregnancy, and the results suggest that low food access, high walkability, and high vacant land in under-resourced neighborhoods are associated with diagnosis of OUD during pregnancy. These findings could help develop complementary tools for universal screening for substance use and provide direction for future studies.

      This systematic review summarized published literature on county-level predictors of drug overdose mortality in the United States (US).
      Peer-reviewed studies and doctoral dissertations published in English between 1990 and July 19, 2022 were identified from PubMed, Web of Science, ProQuest Dissertations & Theses, PsycINFO, CINAHL, and EconLit. Eligible studies examined at least one county-level predictor of drug overdose mortality in US counties. Two reviewers independently completed screening, quality assessment (with an adapted National Institutes of Health Quality Assessment Tool), and data extraction. Results were qualitatively summarized and grouped by predictor categories.
      Of 56 studies included, 42.9% were subnational, and 53.6% were limited to opioid overdose. In multiple studies, measures related to opioid prescribing, illness/disability, economic distress, mining employment, incarceration, family distress, and single-parent families were positively associated with drug overdose mortality outcomes, while measures related to cannabis dispensaries, substance use treatment, social capital, and family households were negatively associated with drug overdose mortality outcomes. Both positive and negative associations were documented for smoking, uninsurance, healthcare professional shortage status, physicians per capita, unemployment, income, poverty, educational attainment, racial composition, and rurality. Findings within studies also differed by subpopulation (by race/ethnicity, gender, age, or rurality) and the type of drugs involved in overdose.
      The findings of this review provide relatively mixed evidence regarding many county-level predictors of overdose mortality, several of which also vary between subpopulations, supporting the importance of additional research to elucidate pathways through which the county context may shape risk of fatal overdose.
